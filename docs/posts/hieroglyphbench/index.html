Can AI read Egyptian Hieroglyphs?

June 22, 2026 1297 words 7 min read

Given a photograph of a column of hieroglyphs, the model outputs the signs it sees as Gardiner sign-list codes (e.g. M17, Y5, N35), in reading order. A Gardiner code names a sign’s type — the owl is G17, the mouth is D21, the seated man is A1 — regardless of which way it faces. I score that sequence using edit distance against an expert transcription of the exact signs in the image.

The Gemini models are far ahead. Gemini 3.5 Flash reads just over half the signs correctly (52.5%), with Gemini 3 Flash Preview (48.8%) and Gemini 3.1 Pro (39.7%) behind it. Everything else is below 23%: GPT-5.5 is the best of the rest at 22.8%, then Kimi K2.6 (19.2%), GPT-5.4 Mini (15.7%), Claude Opus 4.8 (14.0%), MiniMax M3 (11.1%), Qwen 3.7 Plus (10.1%), and GPT-4o (9.5%). Below GPT-5.5 the sign error rate is above 0.8, so those models get most signs wrong.

The smaller Flash models score higher than the larger Pro model, and the largest single gap on the board is between the Gemini models and the rest.

Every reasoning-capable model ran at medium effort. I set it explicitly for all of them except Gemini 3.5 Flash, whose provider default is already medium; GPT-4o has no reasoning setting. The three open-weight models (Kimi K2.6, MiniMax M3, Qwen 3.7 Plus) ran on their first-party providers — Moonshot, MiniMax, and Alibaba — to avoid quantized third-party hosting. Reasoning didn’t close the gap: even at medium effort the non-Gemini models stay well below the Gemini tier. With only 30 inscriptions the means are noisy — re-running the same model moves the score by a few points — so treat small differences as ties.

Examples

To make the scores concrete, here are three inscriptions from the set. Each one shows the photograph the model sees, the expert ground-truth signs, and the readings from the best model (Gemini 3.5 Flash) and a weak one (GPT-4o). I render each reading as the actual hieroglyphs and color them green where they match the ground truth in order and red where they don’t. The Gardiner codes are underneath each strip.

The failure modes are consistent. The strong models track the column sign by sign and slip mostly on signs that look alike — one bird for another, a reed for a forearm. The weak models lose the thread after a sign or two and produce a plausible-looking but unrelated string. A few columns are weathered or packed tightly enough that nothing reads them well.

Dataset construction

The source is the Pyramid of Unas dataset (Morris Franken’s GlyphDataset), built from Alexandre Piankoff’s 1955 photographic plates of the Pyramid Texts in the tomb of the pharaoh Unas (late 5th Dynasty, ~2350 BCE). Every hieroglyph on the ten wall photographs was hand-annotated with its Gardiner code and a bounding box. Because the annotation lives on the photo, the image↔signs pairing is exact by construction.

From this I build column-level inscription items:

  1. Column segmentation. Pyramid Texts are written in vertical columns read top-to-bottom. The signs’ x-centers form a clear comb pattern, so I detect column centers from a histogram of x-positions and assign each sign to the nearest column (splitting the occasional merged double-column by width).
  2. Chunking. Long columns are split into chunks of up to ~20 signs so each item is roughly a sentence or two and the crop stays legible.
  3. Cropping. Each chunk is cropped from the original plate photograph (the bounding box of its signs, plus padding). The ground truth is the ordered list of Gardiner codes in that crop.

This yields 214 single-column inscription crops across all ten plates (~10–29 signs each).

Human curation

The annotations are good but not infallible (the dataset author notes they “may not be 100% correct”), and the automatic column/chunk splitting occasionally produces a crop that’s damaged, ambiguous, or mis-segmented. So every item starts excluded, and I keep good crops by hand in a small review tool that shows the photo beside the ground-truth signs rendered as Unicode hieroglyphs. The leaderboard above is on the first 30 kept items; I’ll expand this over time.

Scoring

The model returns a JSON array of Gardiner codes, enforced with a strict JSON-schema structured output. Scoring is straightforward sequence edit distance — the same idea as character error rate in OCR, but over signs rather than characters:

  • Sign error rate — the number of sign insertions, deletions, and substitutions (Levenshtein distance) needed to turn the predicted Gardiner sequence into the ground truth, divided by the number of ground-truth signs. Lower is better; it can exceed 1 when a model emits far more wrong signs than there are signs to find.
  • Sign accuracy (headline) — 1 − sign error rate, floored at 0, so a prediction with more errors than there are signs scores 0 rather than going negative.

Codes are canonicalized before comparison (G017 → G17, aa1 → Aa1) so formatting differences don’t count as errors. Both metrics are computed per inscription and then macro-averaged, weighting each inscription equally. Random guessing scores ≈ 0, since the sign repertoire is over a thousand glyphs — there is no meaningful chance baseline as there is in a multiple-choice benchmark.

Limitations

  • One monument, one period. Everything here is Old Kingdom Pyramid Text from a single tomb, in one carving style. A model that does well on Unas hasn’t been shown to generalize to, say, Ptolemaic temple reliefs or cursive hieratic. This is the price of using the only openly available expert-annotated photo dataset; broadening it means finding or producing comparable annotations for other monuments.
  • Small sample. 30 curated items makes the means noisy; treat small gaps (e.g. Claude Opus 4.8 vs GPT-5.4 Mini) as ties.
  • Imperfect ground truth. The sign labels are the dataset author’s, not a critical edition. Pyramid Texts are heavily published (Sethe, Allen), so they’re cross-checkable, but some labels will be wrong.
  • Reading order. Column and within-quadrat order is reconstructed from geometry, and the edit-distance metric is order-sensitive — a sign read in the wrong position counts against the score even when the model recognized it correctly. This is a deliberate trade (it keeps the metric simple and OCR-standard) but it means some of the error is reading-order disagreement rather than misrecognition.
